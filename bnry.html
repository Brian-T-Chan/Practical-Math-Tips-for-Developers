Binary Numbers


Image by Felix Mendoza from Pixabay

Curious about what the binary numeral systems discussed in computer science classes and used for certain encodings such as ASCII are all about? Then read this page.

Our numeral system is known as a base 10 numeral system. This is because we use 10 symbols for digits (0, 1, 2, ..., 9). If we arranged the digits we used for counting as shown below and added infinitely many zeros to the left of each number, we get this display.

Scroll down for more numbers.



We notice that when reading the entries in the right-most column, the digits 0, 1, 2, ..., 9 repeat themselves over and over. Looking at the second right-most column, the repeating pattern occurs again, but this time each digit is repeated 10 times before changing to another digit: 0 appears ten times, then 1 appears ten times, ... Looking at the third right-most column, the repeating pattern occurs again, but this time each digit is repeated 10 * 10 times before changing to another digit: 0 appears a hundred times, then 1 appears a hundred times, ...

Now, let's say that we make a display like the above with all of the above patterns except now we are limited to two symbols 0 and 1.

When reading the entries in the right-most column, the digits 0 and 1 repeat themselves over and over. Looking at the second right-most column, the repeating pattern occurs again, but this time each digit is repeated 2 times before changing to another digit: 0 appears two times, then 1 appears two times. Looking at the third right-most column, the repeating pattern occurs again, but this time each digit is repeated 2 * 2 times before changing to another digit: 0 appears four times, then 1 appears four times, ...

Now, compare the above two displays and strip the trailing zeros on the left. We obtain the following.

The symbols to the right give the binary numeral system: zero is written as 0, one is written as 1, two is written as 10, three is written as 11, four is written as 100, and so on.
